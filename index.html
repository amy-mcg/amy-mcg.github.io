Amy McGuire

School of Earth and Environment, University of Leeds a.mcguire@leeds.ac.uk

I am a physical geographer currently working as a Research Fellow in Quaternary Palynology in the School of Earth and Environment at the University of Leeds. Through my work I seek to better understand the nature of vegetation responses to past climate oscillations. In reconstructing past vegetation responses to climatic variability, I seek to answer questions about ecosystem resilience, biodiversity baselines, and the role of humans in altering landscapes, as well as better constraining the magnitude and rate of earth system responses to past warming. In particular, my work aims to improve the dating of palaeoenvironmental records, allowing us to accurately correlate records of past climate change to better understand the spatial variability in ecosystem response.

Employment

University of Leeds

Research Fellow in Quaternary Palynology

I am currently employed as a Postdoctoral Fellow on the ERC funded RISeR project, which aims to better constrain the potential impacts of long-term ice sheet melt by examining marine sediment seqences from the warmer-than-present Last Interglacial (c. 125,000 years ago).

At present, I am examining the pollen contained within Last Interglacial sediments preserved offshore in the North Sea to aid reconstructions of the rates of sea-level change experienced in the region, particularly their relative timing to changes observed in the terrestrial record.

Education

University of Cambridge

PhD Geography

Title: Resolving abrupt palaeoenvironmental changes in a lake sediment sequence from Ioannina, northwest Greece

My PhD thesis sought to reconstruct vegetation responses to abrupt climate change using lake sediments from Ioannina, northwest Greece for the period 45-40 ka BP. This involved production of a new chronology for the sequence using tephra (volcanic ash) layers and the application novel radiocarbon dating methods in addition to sub-centennial scale vegetation reconstruction through high-resolution palynology. In doing so, I sought to develop understanding of the temporal and spatial variation in vegetation responses.

Primary supervisor: Prof Christine Lane (University of Cambridge)
External supervisors: Dr Katy Roucoux and Dr Ian Lawson (University of St. Andrews)
